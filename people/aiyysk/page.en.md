<h3 align = "center">Part I</h3>

Shikai was a lovely trans girl.
Her name came from a transliteration of the Japanese word *isekai* (another world).
The name feels almost like a metaphor:
throughout her short life, she was searching for a world that could belong to her, a world without pain or prejudice, a far shore where she could simply be herself.

If death is not the end of life, but forgetting is, then as long as we still speak the name “Shikai”, she is still here.
She was one of countless trans girls struggling at the edge of survival, looking for somewhere to stay between crowded “sanctuaries” and drifting cities.
Her life was short and painful, yet within one-sided longing and her final bonds, there burned the purest desire to love and be loved.

## Childhood in Black-and-White Photographs

Shikai’s story begins with a yellowing strip of memory.

The warmest scene she remembered was a trip to the supermarket:
her small self in the middle, with her mother and father holding one hand each.
That was the only complete picture of “home” she had left.
But the warmth did not last.
Arguments and beatings became the main rhythm of family life, and in the end, her parents divorced.

She grew up in a single-parent household.
Her father’s extreme need for control, together with repeated domestic violence, cast a shadow over her childhood.
He repeatedly warned her not to return her mother’s calls, cutting off contact between them.
After one fierce conflict, her mother travelled from Guangxi to a village in Yunfu, Guangdong.
It was the last time Shikai remembered feeling the warmth of a mother’s love.
Her mother held her, bought her plenty of food and clothes, and then was gone again.
Her father took the things with visible disdain, as though even that tenderness had to pass through him first.
After that, her mother disappeared from her life entirely.

## The Girl Who Collected Scrap, and the Flash of Light

Her father gambled heavily, and the family was left in deep poverty.
In Shikai’s memory, childhood meant going through the streets of Guangzhou with her grandmother, collecting garbage and selling scrap.
Her grandmother was the one person she could rely on in those grey years. She worked as a school cleaner and would not drink the milk given out at school, saving it all for Shikai instead.
A kind principal, moved by their circumstances, helped arrange an exemption from school fees.

Suffering forced her to grow up too early.
In the summer after elementary school, she went out early every day to collect scrap, just as she had when she was younger, hoping to save a little money.
Over two months she managed to put aside two hundred yuan.
That small sum was her own quiet act of resistance.
She loved computers and circuits, and used the money to buy a mobile phone, borrowing internet access wherever she could to study circuitry and networking.
She once said, “I live by the internet. Without it, I am nothing.”

Her father kept a memory card containing photographs from her childhood, including photos with her mother.
Those images were proof that those days had existed at all, but he would not give the card to her.
She could only watch those memories wear away in her mind, until all that remained were a few fixed black-and-white frames.

## Glimmers of Light and Mould-Stained Uniforms

When I met her in 2022, she was attending a junior high school in Yunfu, Guangdong.
By then, she had already taught herself programming and web development, studied electronics and phone repair, and was taking online jobs to earn money.
She was astonishingly capable, full of talent — the kind of growth that comes from forcing a branch out of barren ground.

But even such a tender shoot still had to endure the storm.
Bullying followed her at school like a shadow.
Some classmates borrowed money from her and never paid it back; some even turned around and accused her of stealing.
She was kind to a fault, always wanting to help others even when she herself had almost nothing, and what she received in return was malice.

Thankfully, there was still one faint glimmer of light.
Her chemistry teacher, seeing how poor she was, would sometimes call her into the office, quietly pass on fruit that had been given to the teachers, and speak to her with patience and care.
Those moments were among the few chances she had to breathe at school.

At the time, her father was working away from home, so she was not living in her own village but staying with her aunt’s family.
They were poor as well.
A car accident had wiped out what little savings they had, and her uncle did not treat her kindly.
Food was rationed tightly.
If she secretly fried herself an egg, she would be scolded; at her worst, she survived on rice with soy sauce.
In Guangdong’s damp climate, even her school uniform carried mould stains that could never quite be washed out.

But what suffocated her most was her father’s surveillance.
Though he was far away, he had installed a remote camera in her room and watched her movements every day like a ghost.
That feeling of being watched, with nowhere to hide, frightened her more than poverty itself.

## A Suffocating Dormitory, and a Dream Threatened with Destruction

After finishing junior high school, she was admitted to a vocational school to study computing.
She thought it might be a new beginning.
With money she had earned herself, she slowly pieced together a computer to bring with her to the dormitory.
It was precious to her — not just a machine, but a companion, the one clear window she had onto the world.

When her father found out, he scolded her harshly.
To him, the computer was something dangerous, almost monstrous, and he flatly forbade her to bring it.
She tried to resist.
A few days later, he sent a final warning: if she did not obey, he would come to the school himself and smash the computer in front of everyone.

In that dormitory, twelve people were packed into one room.
The air felt stale.
Privacy did not exist. Her classmates at the vocational school were even more oppressive than before.
The bullying continued, and malice spread everywhere like mould in damp weather.

Then, on 15 December 2023, near the end of that suffocating term, she finally made a choice:
she began HRT and took her first step towards becoming herself.
It was the first time she had seized control of her own body.
It was also her last declaration of war against the old world.

## Escape and the Sanctuary

As she grew older, she had to bear not only violence at home, but also bullying at school, abuse online, and the depression, dissociation, and severe somatic distress that followed.
For Shikai, running away was not an impulsive act. It was a breakout forced by the need to survive.

Lunar New Year’s Eve in 2024, the day when families were supposed to gather together, became the beginning of her escape.
Trapped between her father’s control and ongoing conflict at home, she planned her departure carefully.
In the early hours of 19 February, beneath dim streetlights, she lifted a heavy suitcase and finally took that step.
She arrived at a place known as “the Sanctuary”: A temporary refuge for many girls like her, with several people crowded into each bedroom.
It was cramped and makeshift, but it was also the only place where she could breathe.

In that crowded space, Shikai formed new bonds.
Three people slept on the same bed, and one of them was Sakurana, the friend she had come to find.
Those days were among the warmest in her life, and also among the most powerless.
She had no money at all.
Sakurana would come back from work and bring her a meal.
Shikai once said to me, full of guilt, “I owe her for this. I have to pay her back…”

But what she wanted to repay was not just the cost of a meal. It was a promise to stay alive.

When Shikai tried to use self-harm to ease her anxiety, Sakurana threatened her in return: “If I find out you’ve done it again, I’ll kill myself.”
Later, when Shikai told me about this, she said, “If she’s gone, then there’d be no reason for me to stay alive either.”

And yet, beneath that bond of mutual dependence lay Shikai’s deepest loneliness.
In one of her posts, she wrote: “Shikai likes Sakurana, but Sakurana doesn’t like Shikai.”
Sakurana was Shikai’s source of comfort, the pillar that kept her standing, but Shikai knew she was not that for Sakurana.
This imbalance made the warmth she received all the more painful.
The more she depended on it, the more afraid she became of turning into a burden.
She tried, carefully and quietly, to make herself useful to Sakurana, as though by helping her she might earn the right to stay.
But the love she longed for most remained out of reach.

Even in that closeness, she was still fighting the darkness inside herself.
Dissociation and physical pain tormented her constantly.
She misused medication in secret, vomiting for a long time afterwards, and yet described it to me like this: “It felt really good.
All the bad thoughts, the anxiety, everything in my head just disappeared…
Anxiety is the thing that troubles me most, and it troubles other people too.”

She once told me, “It’s not really the environment itself that I mind.
I just keep longing for a beautiful future, and then I feel powerless, disappointed, in pain, and hopeless.” She was always like that.
Even when she herself was suffering, she worried first about becoming trouble to other people.
She envied others, dreamed of a better future, and yet was wrapped layer upon layer in helplessness.
Her wishes were simple and painfully modest: to find work, to save some money, to buy Sakurana a gift.
She wanted to become a girl who could take care of others.
Perhaps because she understood pain so deeply, she wanted to offer love in return.

## The Long Descent into Dusk

By the middle of 2024, the walls of reality were closing in.
Distance did not free her from her father’s shadow.
Over the phone, he pressed her with accusatory questions — “Have you started taking estrogen injections?” — and that specific, invasive scrutiny hung above her like a blade.
At the same time, poverty gave her no room to rest.
Once her money was gone, she had to depend on the online world for survival, taking whatever work she could find over the internet just to keep this fragile life going.

Her wishes were so humble that they ached.
In March, she wrote in a post: “I really want a little dress, but I can’t afford one.”
It was the plainest kind of desire, a girl wanting something pretty of her own, and even that felt impossibly far away.
At the same time, severe sleep problems and gender dysphoria began to consume her.
She described it like this: “My body feels unbearably disgusting.
Every time it gets bad, I lose a little more of my emotional awareness.”

Even when she was penniless and barely holding herself together, she still held on to an almost stubborn kindness.
When she saw someone online trying to exploit pity for money, even though that person could have given her a place to stay, she still could not help speaking up: “Other people’s money is money too.”
She did not want to become a burden, and she could not bear to watch kindness being taken advantage of.

When someone asked her how much longer she could endure life like this, her answer carried the weight of despair: “I don’t know whether I can make it to eighteen.”
It was like standing in a tunnel with no end in sight.

By July, the pressure of rent had become the final straw. Under the weight of survival, her words began to break apart.

> “What have you been up to lately?”
>
> “Waiting to die. If it’s possible, could I live with you in future?”
>
> “Of course. You could stay here all the time if you needed.”
>
> “Could I eat your leftovers?”
>
> “How could you say that?”
>
> “Maybe… before long, no one will be able to see me any more.”

Those few lines of conversation now read like a rehearsal for her departure.
It was not that she did not want to live.
It was that living itself had become too heavy to bear.
Afterwards, she bought stronger medication, trying to force down the storm inside her, only to end up spending whole days in a haze.

Shikai loved Sakurana.
Sakurana was the pillar holding up her spirit, but she knew with painful clarity that the feeling was not returned in the same way.
That sorrow, together with her body’s total exhaustion, left her more drained than ever.
She began to dissociate, feeling as if her soul were floating outside her own body, unable to accept that the flesh she lived in was really hers.

The girl who once wanted to buy a gift for a friend, who once wanted to know what a sweet little life with someone she loved might feel like, was slowly losing her light.

## Her Last Companion

In the end, Shikai left the Sanctuary and went to Shanghai, where she found Mofcandy.
It would be the final stop in her journey through life.
Mofcandy was renting a place in Shanghai and surviving on day labour and odd jobs, and she took in the long-drifting Shikai.

In September, Mofcandy sent a message: “If I go back to school, it’ll be very hard to arrange somewhere for her to stay.
Right now, she’s the only reason I still want to live.”

Fate bound the two lonely souls tightly together.
Mofcandy never returned to school.
In that enormous city, they may still have been poor, still on the margins, but at least they had each other.

There was no way forward in reality, and the way out inside themselves had already closed.
For Shikai, perhaps that “place without darkness” was the only destination left to them.

## A Meeting Where There Is No Darkness

In the early hours of 15 December 2024, before the winter wind had fully pressed through the window frame, two lives quietly met.

> “Are you… alright… in the other world?”
>
> “I… I’ll be there soon to see you.”
>
> “Oh… no, we’ve already met… the final promise has been fulfilled…”

These were timed messages she had written the night before, messages addressed to the already departed Mofcandy, and to the world she was about to leave.
At the moment they were sent, Shikai and Mofcandy met at the end of life and went to keep that “final promise”.
This time, they would not be parted.

Shikai once said to me, “I’m not afraid of death. Death just means everything ends.”
But I would rather believe that, in her last moments, she was still holding on to something else as well: the wish to change, to heal, to love someone, to save enough to buy a friend a gift, or even simply to own a little dress of her own.

Now, the child who looked for her mother inside fading black-and-white memories, the girl who still held on to dreams of computers while picking through scrap, the girl worn raw by illness, has finally laid down every burden she carried.
At last, she has slipped free of her father’s suffocating control.
She no longer has to tremble beneath a roof filled with violence and shouting.
She no longer has to collect scrap in the street in order to survive.
She no longer has to fight that deep, gnawing anxiety and pain with medication.

The words she left behind, those small traces of longing, of wanting to love and be loved, did not vanish when her account went dark.

Goodnight, Shikai.
May there be no anxiety where you are now, no displacement, no shadow of violence, only the gentle and everlasting peace she longed for.
The photograph her father refused to give her may one day fade, but the memory kept here will remain alive.

<h3 align = "center">Part II</h3>

She was not only the girl struggling in the current of fate, but also, in the eyes of her friends, an ordinary child who loved flashing devices, spoke gently, and would send “pat pat ❤️” late at night.

In Shinjo Kurumi’s memory, she appears in more concrete detail: through the electronic devices she loved, the engineering phone she spent two months trying to fix, and the tense, breathless hours of that New Year’s Eve.

## First Encounter

I met her through a post on CoolAPK where she was asking for help.

It was in the autumn of 2023. She had a Qualcomm Reference Design prototype on the Snapdragon 855 platform, and after a failed flashing attempt, it would no longer boot.
She had no choice but to ask for help.
I left a comment saying I might be able to assist, and soon afterwards we added each other on QQ.

After hearing her describe the problem, I realized she had made the same mistake I once had: she had switched the system slot[^1].
On those prototype devices, slot B was empty, which meant the phone could no longer boot and had fallen into EDL mode[^2].

As we talked, I happened to learn that her life was not going well.
When I tried to ask more, though, she stopped me. She said, “Just take care of yourself. Don’t worry about me.”

Even so, later on she told me that she had long been living with domestic violence, and that she had already tried running away from home more than once.

When the phone finally lit up and booted again, she was so happy.
She said, “I’ve been struggling with this thing for two whole months. Thanks to you, it’s finally working.”

It made me genuinely happy to know that, in some small way, I had managed to help someone.

As we kept talking, she told me that she was MTF, and sent me a photo. In it I could see boxes of medicines such as Progynova and cyproterone acetate. I was caught off guard, and asked again.

Me: “You’re… joking, right?”

Shikai: “No.”

Me: “So that means… you’re MTF?”

Shikai: “I am. What about it?”

Me: “I used to think MTF girls were hard to approach. Maybe that was just a wrong impression I got from what I’d seen online.”

Shikai: “Everyone is different. You just feel they’re hard to approach because you get nervous talking to them, and don’t know what to say.”

Me: “I’d never actually talked to an MTF person before. I only knew the group existed. I was afraid that my wording might hurt them, so I kept my distance — out of respect, really, but also because I didn’t know how to approach.”

Shikai: “That makes sense. I’m like that too.”

It was the first time I had ever spoken with an MTF person.
As a cis, straight guy, I could not really process it all at once.
For two or three days, I did not reply to her messages while I tried to sort out my thoughts.
Then, once I was ready, I gathered my courage and started talking to her again.

Shikai: “Where did you go?”

Me: “Sorry.”

Shikai: “Hey, it’s alright. *Hugs* ❤️”

Me: “My head’s been full of dark thoughts these last couple of days. I was afraid they’d affect you, so I stayed quiet.”

Shikai: “No, not at all. It just made me worry about you more.”

Me: “Honestly, the whole thing felt so surreal that I could hardly believe it…”

Shikai: “What’s wrong? Pat pat.”

Me: “Like I said before, I always thought MTF girls were hard to approach, and I didn’t know how to speak without accidentally hurting them.”

Shikai: “Just treat us normally~ I understand what you mean~”

Me: “The problem is that what most people think of as ‘normal’ may not actually apply here.”

Shikai: “I wouldn’t make things difficult for a friend. Just talk to me normally, that’s enough. And if there’s anything you need, we can work through it together~ Pat pat, silly.”

She was so gentle, so full of warmth. From then on, I truly accepted her into my heart.

She became one of my closest friends.

## Running Away

9 February 2024 was Lunar New Year’s Eve.
At around seven in the evening, my family had already finished dinner and were sitting together, talking about the year that was about to pass.
As one of the younger people there, I could hardly join in, so like everyone else my age I sat scrolling through my phone.

A few days earlier, two people in Shikai’s group chat had got into a fierce argument.
I had been affected by it more than I wanted to admit and felt deeply uneasy.
But Shikai herself did not seem especially disturbed.
She said, “It’s just one of those things. There’s nothing to be scared of. Let it be.” So I told her what was on my mind.

Me: “I’m really scared. It’s because of my anxiety. I’m afraid people will dislike me, and I’m afraid of seeing other people be disliked too.”

Shikai: “The best thing is to focus on doing your own part first~”

Me: “Sigh… A friend once described me as being like Sayaka from *Puella Magi Madoka Magica* — too emotional.”

Shikai: “It’s alright. You’ll get used to it slowly. Pat pat~”

Me: “That’s good. Whenever my anxiety gets bad and I’m scared, hearing someone say ‘it’s okay’ really does make me feel better. I’m such an idiot.jpg”

Shikai: *(a sticker that looked rather downcast)*

Me: “What’s wrong? Did something upsetting happen?”

Shikai: “No. I get anxious too. Just not right now. Right now I’m looking at an idiot!”

Me: “I’m the idiot, hehe.”

Shikai: “Alright, alright~”

Just like that, my worries faded. Then I suddenly remembered — it was New Year’s Eve. I ought to wish her a happy new year.

Me: “Mm. Happy New Year — a little early.”

Shikai: “I don’t really like the New Year.”

Given what she had gone through, that was not hard to understand.

Me: “Alright.”

Shikai: “I’m running away. Leaving home. Don’t worry, I’ll be alright. Goodnight.”

Me: “…What?”

Shikai: “[Auto-reply] So lonely[^3]”

Me: “Are you really okay…?”

Shikai: “I’m okay.”

Me: “Alright. Please stay safe.”

Shikai: “Worried.”

Me: “What’s wrong? Did something happen?”

Shikai: “It’s a matter of timing. I don’t know if I’ll be able to get out. Can you read my posts?”

Me: “Yes, I can. Let me have a look… I see. It’s still the conflict with your family’s traditional views, right?”

Shikai: “Mm.”

Me: “I saw the three posts. Two were about your family not understanding, and the other was just someone mindlessly attacking you.”

Shikai: “Ignore the troll.”

Me: “Sorry. I don’t really know what to do in a situation like this. People’s traditional beliefs aren’t something they can just throw away overnight. All I can do is hope you stay safe.”

Shikai: “The main issue is timing. My dad starts work on the 19th and leaves on the evening of the 18th. If school begins on the 19th, boarding students go in on the evening of the 18th, and day students go in on the morning of the 19th. Right now we’re still in the countryside, and he’ll take me out of the county at noon on the 18th. If he leaves earlier, maybe I’ll be able to slip away.”

Me: “Mm… so you really have to plan carefully. I can’t do much to help, but… please, just stay safe.”

At 21:30 on 18 February, only a few hours remained before she meant to leave. It was also her birthday. To mark it, I wrote these lines and sent them to the group chat:

> Across the past and the future  
> when sunlight falls into my heart  
> I can finally walk out of the shadows  
>
> Once more, tears rise to my eyes  
> only because I have regained  
> the courage to embrace love

“Anyway, the reason I wrote this poem is that I’m truly grateful to have met all of you. I was someone who had always longed for love and understanding, yet was never welcomed…”

But Shikai was not doing especially well that night. She said she had rhinitis, a sore throat, and inflamed gums. At 23:18, she complained in the group chat:

Shikai: “Why is my aunt like this? The light in the living room is on, so of course it looks like my room is lit too. She says I turned the light on and told me to switch it off straight away.”

“I’m trying to figure out how to move my suitcase downstairs later. It’s so heavy.”

Me: “You should have moved it down bit by bit in advance.”

Shikai: “People are watching at home.”

At 00:56 on 19 February, it was already past midnight.

Group Member A: “Kaikai’s about to make her move, isn’t she?”

Shikai: “My family still isn’t asleep. They’re all still active in the living room.”

Group Member A: “Ah? Wait until two or three. Find a moment when they’ve all gone to bed.”

Group Member B: “Are your preparations done? Do you really have the resolve for this?”

Shikai: “Everything’s ready. It’s just… why aren’t they going to sleep yet…”

At 02:11, she sent several photos to the group. Under the dim yellow streetlights, she was carrying her suitcase. And then she left.

## Her Departure

At the time, I was in hospital receiving treatment, so I know very little about this part.
It is a painful memory, and I do not wish to ask those who were there for more details.

All I know is that, at midnight on 15 December 2024, after learning that another MTF girl had died a few days earlier, she and another MTF girl she was living with took a large quantity of drugs and chemical solution, and left this world.

> “A lifetime of friendship embracing love,  
> a whole life of beautiful scenes in full bloom.”

Goodnight. I hope that in another life, you can be a carefree and lovely girl.

### References and Notes

[^1]: Android’s seamless update mechanism uses two sets of partitions called slots (usually slot A and slot B) to prevent devices from becoming unbootable when a system update fails. For more information, see [Google’s official documentation](https://source.android.com/docs/core/ota/ab).

[^2]: EDL stands for Qualcomm’s built-in “Emergency Download Mode”, which can be used for firmware recovery. It is also commonly known as “9008 mode”.

[^3]: This was an automatic reply set in QQ for Away or Busy status. It appeared more than once later on and is not repeated in the notes each time.
